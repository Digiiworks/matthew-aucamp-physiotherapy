Back Pain

Back Pain Treatment in Gqeberha: Evidence-Based Solutions That Work

April 7, 2026·5 min read·By Matthew Aucamp

If you're reading this, there's a good chance you've been living with back pain for longer than you'd like to admit. You're not alone. Back pain is the leading cause of disability worldwide — and one of the most common reasons patients walk through my clinic door here in Walmer, Gqeberha.

The frustrating truth is that most back pain is poorly understood, poorly explained, and poorly treated. Patients are often told to "just rest", handed a prescription for painkillers, and sent on their way. Sometimes they're told their back is "out", their spine is "crooked", or that they have a "weak core". None of those explanations actually help — and most of them aren't even accurate.

Why Most People Wait Too Long to Get Help

Back pain has a strange way of making people defer care. We tell ourselves it'll settle on its own. We avoid the activities that hurt. We try a couple of stretches we found online. We take ibuprofen. And for a while, things sometimes do settle — until the next flare-up comes along, usually worse than the last.

The problem with this cycle is that pain becomes the only signal we listen to, when it's actually one of the least reliable signals the body produces. By the time someone arrives at my practice for chronic back pain, they've often had it for months or years — long after the original tissue irritation has healed. What they're left with is a sensitised nervous system, weakened movement patterns, and the belief that their back is fragile.

What Actually Causes Back Pain?

Most back pain is mechanical. That means it comes from how the spine is loaded, moved and held — not from a structural problem like a herniated disc or nerve damage. The four most common drivers I see in clinic are:

  • Postural overload — long hours sitting or standing in positions the body wasn't designed for
  • Movement avoidance — patients move less when their back hurts, which makes it worse
  • Muscular imbalance — some muscles do too much work, others switch off
  • Disc-related irritation — sometimes legitimate, but far less common than people think

The encouraging news is that mechanical back pain responds extremely well to the right physiotherapy. The discouraging news is that most patients have been treated as if they have something more serious — and have been given advice that makes the situation worse rather than better.

Why Painkillers Alone Don't Fix Back Pain

Painkillers have a role. They can take the edge off an acute flare-up so you can sleep, move and start rehabilitation. But they don't address the underlying mechanical drivers, and the moment you stop taking them, the pain comes back.

Worse, painkillers can mask warning signals from the body — encouraging you to do more than your spine is ready for, and setting you up for the next flare-up. They are a tool, not a treatment.

The DBC Approach: Why It Works

I am one of the few physiotherapists in the Eastern Cape with a DBC Certified Back Expert qualification. DBC stands for Dynamic Back Care — a Finnish-developed, internationally recognised rehabilitation system used in over 30 countries.

What makes DBC work where other approaches fail is the combination of three things:

  1. Detailed clinical assessment — identifying the actual mechanical drivers of your pain
  2. Progressive loading — graded exercise that rebuilds the strength and tolerance the spine needs
  3. Patient education — so you understand exactly what is happening and what you can do about it

DBC isn't a magic bullet. It is hard work, it takes between six and twelve weeks, and it requires consistent attendance. But it has more research evidence behind it than almost any other approach to chronic back pain — and the outcomes for patients who commit to it are consistently excellent.

What Your First Appointment Looks Like

When you first come to my Walmer practice, we'll spend a full hour together. I'll take a detailed history — how the pain started, what makes it better or worse, what you've already tried, and what your goals are. Then I'll perform a thorough physical assessment of your spine, posture, joint movement and muscle function.

By the end of that first session, you'll understand exactly what is going on, what we are going to do about it, and roughly how long it should take. You'll usually receive some hands-on treatment in the same session, and you'll leave with a small set of exercises to start straight away.

Things You Can Start Doing Today

  • Move more, not less — gentle walking is one of the most effective things you can do
  • Avoid prolonged sitting — get up every 30–45 minutes, even just for a stretch
  • Sleep in a position that feels neutral — usually side-lying with a pillow between the knees
  • Stop catastrophising — the spine is far more resilient than you've been led to believe
  • Get assessed properly — guesswork is what got most patients into trouble in the first place

When Back Pain Is a Red Flag

Most back pain is mechanical and not dangerous, but there are some symptoms that warrant urgent assessment. Seek care immediately if you experience any of the following:

  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Numbness in the saddle area
  • Progressive weakness in the legs
  • Severe pain at night that doesn't respond to position
  • Unexplained weight loss alongside back pain

If you're in Gqeberha or Port Elizabeth and any of these apply, please go directly to your GP or emergency department.
