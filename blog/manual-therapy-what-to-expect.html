Manual Therapy

What is Manual Therapy? A Gqeberha Physiotherapist Explains What to Expect

April 7, 2026·4 min read·By Matthew Aucamp

"Manual therapy" is one of those phrases that sounds vaguely clinical but doesn't actually tell you very much. Patients often arrive at my Walmer clinic unsure whether they're going to get a massage, a chiropractic-style adjustment, or something they need to be nervous about. The honest answer is: none of the above — and all of the above, depending on what your body needs.

This article explains what manual therapy actually is, the techniques it includes, what conditions it helps, and what your first session will feel like.

Manual Therapy Is Not Just Massage

Massage is one component of soft-tissue work, and soft-tissue work is one component of manual therapy. But manual therapy is much broader. It is a clinical, hands-on approach to assessing and treating the muscles, joints, ligaments and connective tissues of the body — informed by detailed anatomical knowledge and current research.

A massage therapist works with relaxation and general muscle tension. A physiotherapist using manual therapy works with specific clinical findings — a stiff joint, an irritable muscle, a movement restriction, a sensitised nerve — and applies techniques targeted at those specific issues.

The Core Manual Therapy Techniques

Manual therapy includes a wide range of techniques, but the most commonly used in musculoskeletal physiotherapy fall into four broad categories:

Joint Mobilisation

Gentle, rhythmic movement applied to a stiff joint to restore its range of motion. It is the bread and butter of manual therapy and works particularly well for joints that have lost mobility through injury, immobilisation or postural overload.

Joint Manipulation

A faster, smaller-amplitude technique applied to a joint at the end of its available range. This is the technique that sometimes produces the audible "pop" people associate with chiropractic care. Used selectively and appropriately, it can produce immediate improvements in pain and range. It is not used on every patient, and it is not a substitute for the broader rehabilitation programme.

Soft Tissue Release

Techniques applied directly to muscles and connective tissue — including trigger-point release, muscle-energy techniques and instrument-assisted approaches. These are particularly useful for muscular over-activity, persistent tension and post-injury stiffness.

Myofascial Release

Slower, sustained pressure applied to fascial restrictions. The fascia is the connective tissue web that runs throughout the body, and when it becomes restricted it can pull movement patterns out of alignment far from the site of the original problem.

Conditions That Respond Well to Manual Therapy

  • Acute joint stiffness and limited range of motion
  • Chronic neck pain and cervicogenic headaches
  • Lower back pain — both acute and chronic
  • Soft-tissue tightness following sports injury
  • Frozen shoulder and other capsular conditions
  • Postural dysfunction in desk-based workers

Note that manual therapy is rarely the only treatment used. It works best alongside graded exercise, patient education and movement re-training. On its own, manual therapy provides relief that lasts a few days. Combined with the right rehabilitation, those gains become permanent.

What Your First Session Feels Like

A typical first session starts with a detailed conversation about your symptoms, history and goals. From there I'll perform a physical examination — checking posture, joint movement, muscle function and any specific orthopaedic tests required to confirm a working diagnosis.

If manual therapy is appropriate, I'll explain exactly what I'm going to do and why before any hands-on work begins. The techniques themselves should never be painful — they may feel firm, and you may feel a stretch or a release, but you should never grit your teeth through them. If something is uncomfortable, you tell me, and we adjust.

Most patients leave the first session with noticeably better movement and reduced pain. You may feel slightly tender or "worked" the next day, much like after a good gym session — that's normal and usually settles within 24 hours.

How Many Sessions Do You Need?

It depends entirely on what we're treating. Some acute conditions resolve in two or three sessions. Chronic conditions usually take six to twelve. The goal is always to get you to a point where you can manage things yourself — not to keep you coming back indefinitely.

If you don't see clear, measurable progress within the first three or four sessions, something needs to change in the treatment plan. That's the honest answer — and I'd rather have that conversation early than waste your time and money.

Manual Therapy vs Exercise: Why Both Together Work Best

Patients sometimes ask whether they should just stick to exercise and skip the hands-on work. The answer in most cases is no — not because exercise alone doesn't help, but because manual therapy makes the exercise far more effective. By restoring range of motion and reducing pain in the early stages, manual therapy creates the conditions for rehabilitation exercise to actually work.

Think of it like this: manual therapy unlocks the door, and exercise walks you through it. You need both.

Is Manual Therapy Safe?

When delivered by a qualified physiotherapist, manual therapy has an excellent safety profile. Side effects are usually limited to mild post-treatment soreness. Serious adverse events are extremely rare and almost always preventable through proper screening and clinical reasoning — which is exactly what your physiotherapist's training is designed to provide.

If you have any concerns about a specific technique, your physiotherapist should be more than happy to explain it, demonstrate it gently first, or use an alternative approach. Consent is ongoing.

The Conditions Manual Therapy Helps Most

Manual therapy isn't a one-trick tool, and it isn't right for every patient — but for the conditions it suits, the response can be remarkable. In my Walmer clinic, the conditions that consistently respond best are mechanical neck pain and stiffness, lower back joint restriction, frozen shoulder in its later stages, tension-type headaches driven by upper cervical dysfunction, lateral hip pain, tennis and golfer's elbow, and post-surgical stiffness once the surgeon has cleared loaded movement. Across all of these, manual therapy works best when it's used to create a window of opportunity — a temporary improvement in pain and movement that allows the patient to load and rehabilitate more effectively in the same session and over the following days.

The conditions where manual therapy plays a much smaller role include acute inflammatory flare-ups, conditions driven primarily by deconditioning rather than mechanical restriction, and any situation where the priority is education and graded loading rather than passive treatment. A good physiotherapist knows the difference, and won't reach for hands-on work just because a patient expects it.

What Patients Often Get Wrong About Manual Therapy

The biggest misconception I encounter is the idea that manual therapy is "putting things back into place." Joints don't go in and out. Vertebrae don't slip. Ribs don't dislocate when you sneeze. The popping sound during a manipulation isn't bone moving — it's a harmless gas release inside the joint capsule. What manual therapy actually does is modulate the nervous system, reduce protective muscle tone, improve joint glide, and create a brief window in which movement feels easier and less threatening. That window is the opportunity. What you do with it — the loading, the exercise, the changes to your daily habits — is what creates lasting recovery.
