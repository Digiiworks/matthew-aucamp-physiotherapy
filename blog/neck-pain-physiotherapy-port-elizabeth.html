Neck Pain

Neck Pain Physiotherapy in Port Elizabeth — A DBC Certified Method That Works

April 7, 2026·5 min read·By Matthew Aucamp

Neck pain has quietly become one of the most common conditions I see in clinic. It used to be considered an "older person's complaint", but the truth is that the typical neck pain patient walking into my Walmer practice today is in their thirties or forties — and often has a desk job, a smartphone, and a steadily worsening problem.

This article explains why neck pain is so prevalent in Port Elizabeth and Gqeberha right now, why it's so often poorly treated, and what an evidence-based DBC certified approach actually looks like.

Why Neck Pain Is Everywhere

The cervical spine — the seven vertebrae that make up your neck — is a remarkable piece of biological engineering. It supports a head that weighs roughly the same as a bowling ball, while still allowing the kind of fine, precise movement you need to drive, read, scan a room, or look up at a friend.

The trade-off is that the cervical spine is exquisitely sensitive to sustained loading. Hours spent in a forward-head, slumped-shoulder posture — typing, scrolling, driving — places loads on the deep neck stabilisers and posterior chain that they were never designed to manage. Over time, those tissues fatigue, the joints stiffen, and the local musculature becomes overactive in a way that produces persistent pain.

It's not weakness, it's not "bad posture" in any moral sense, and it's not a deformity. It is mechanical overload — and it is reversible.

Cervical Spine Anatomy in Plain Language

Your neck has seven vertebrae stacked on top of one another, separated by intervertebral discs that act as shock absorbers. Around them are dozens of small joints, several layers of muscle, ligaments, nerves and blood vessels. The deep stabiliser muscles work like a precision suspension system — when they switch off, the larger superficial muscles take over and quickly become irritable.

Most chronic neck pain is not coming from "a pinched nerve" or "a slipped disc". It is coming from a combination of irritated joint capsules, sensitised muscles, and disrupted movement patterns. The treatment, therefore, is to restore the mechanics — not to chase the pain with anti-inflammatories.

Why Neck Pain Is So Often Misdiagnosed

I see patients almost every week who have been told their neck pain is from "a trapped nerve", "muscle spasm" or "a degenerative spine". Sometimes they've been sent for X-rays or MRIs that showed "wear and tear" or "disc bulges" and have walked out feeling that their spine is broken.

Here's the inconvenient truth that the research has shown for over a decade: most asymptomatic adults over 30 have disc bulges and degenerative changes on imaging. These findings correlate poorly with pain. Telling a patient their neck is "degenerating" usually does more harm than good — it teaches them their body is fragile, which is the single biggest predictor of chronic pain becoming entrenched.

What DBC Neck Certification Actually Means

DBC (Dynamic Back Care) is a Finnish-developed, internationally recognised rehabilitation system used in over 30 countries. The cervical certification involves an intensive postgraduate training programme, supervised clinical hours, and a formal competency assessment. It is held by very few physiotherapists in the Eastern Cape.

What DBC offers patients is a structured, measurable, evidence-based path through neck rehabilitation. It combines:

  • Detailed cervical assessment — including outcome measures so we can track real progress
  • Manual therapy — hands-on joint mobilisation and soft tissue work
  • Progressive resistance training — graded loading of the deep stabilisers
  • Patient education — so you understand exactly what we are doing and why

The protocol typically runs over six to twelve weeks. By the end of it, most patients have not just less pain but better function — and the tools to keep it that way.

Manual Therapy for the Neck

Hands-on treatment remains one of the most effective tools we have for neck pain. Joint mobilisation, soft-tissue release, and trigger-point work can produce immediate, measurable improvements in pain and range of motion.

But manual therapy on its own is short-lived. The relief lasts a few days, and then the same mechanical drivers reassert themselves. Manual therapy only delivers lasting change when it is paired with the right loading exercises and the right patient education — which is exactly how the DBC protocol uses it.

Postural Correction and Home Exercises

The phrase "fix your posture" is one of my least favourite things in physiotherapy. Posture is not a fixed thing, and there is no such thing as a "perfect" posture you should hold all day. The healthiest position is the next one. The goal isn't rigidity — it's variability.

That said, there are simple changes that almost every desk-based patient benefits from:

  • Move every 30 minutes — set a timer if you have to
  • Raise your screen — top of the monitor at eye level
  • Drop your shoulders — most desk workers hold them braced upward all day without realising
  • Add deep neck flexor exercises — gentle, specific, and remarkably effective

When Neck Pain Is a Red Flag

Most neck pain is mechanical and not dangerous, but some symptoms warrant urgent assessment:

  • Numbness, tingling or weakness in the arms or hands
  • Clumsiness, balance changes or coordination problems
  • Severe night pain unresponsive to position
  • Neck pain following significant trauma
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever alongside neck pain

If any of these apply, please contact your GP urgently before booking a physiotherapy appointment.
