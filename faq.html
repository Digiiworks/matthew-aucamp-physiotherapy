Getting Started

Do I need a doctor's referral to see a physiotherapist in South Africa?

No. Physiotherapists in South Africa are first-contact practitioners, which means you can book an appointment directly without a referral from your GP. Most medical aids will still cover your treatment under physiotherapy benefits, regardless of whether you have a referral letter.

What should I bring to my first physiotherapy appointment?

Bring any relevant scans, X-rays or reports, your medical aid card, a list of any medications you're currently taking, and wear loose comfortable clothing that allows easy access to the area being assessed. Shorts and a t-shirt work well for most lower-body or back assessments.

How long is a physiotherapy appointment?

First appointments are 60 minutes — long enough for a thorough history, physical examination, working diagnosis, and the start of treatment. Follow-up sessions are typically 30 to 45 minutes, depending on your specific treatment needs.

How many physiotherapy sessions will I need?

It depends entirely on the condition. Acute injuries often resolve in 2 to 4 sessions. Chronic conditions and structured DBC spinal rehabilitation programmes typically run for 6 to 12 sessions over several weeks. You will receive a clear treatment plan and realistic timeline at your first appointment, and we re-assess progress regularly.

About Your Treatment

What is DBC certified physiotherapy?

DBC stands for Dynamic Back Care — a Finnish-developed, internationally recognised spinal rehabilitation system used by leading physiotherapy practices in over 30 countries. Matthew holds dual DBC certifications for both neck and back rehabilitation, making him one of the few practitioners in the Eastern Cape qualified in both protocols.

Learn more about DBC certified rehabilitation →

Does manual therapy hurt?

Manual therapy should never be painful. You may feel firm pressure, a stretch, or a release sensation — but the techniques are always within your comfort tolerance. Some patients experience mild post-treatment soreness similar to after a workout, which usually settles within 24 hours. If anything feels uncomfortable during treatment, you tell Matthew and he adjusts.

Can physiotherapy help me avoid back surgery?

For many patients with mechanical back pain — including some with disc-related conditions — yes. The DBC spinal rehabilitation protocol has strong evidence behind it as an alternative to surgery in appropriate cases. Matthew will assess your specific situation and refer you to a surgeon only if it is genuinely indicated. Read more about back pain treatment →

What conditions does Matthew treat?

Matthew treats musculoskeletal conditions including back pain, neck pain, sports injuries, post-operative recovery, joint pain, postural dysfunction, headaches with cervical origin, and chronic spinal conditions. As a dual DBC Certified specialist, he has particular expertise in chronic neck and back rehabilitation.

Common Conditions

How do I know if my back pain needs physiotherapy?

If back pain has lasted more than a week, keeps coming back, or is interfering with sleep, work or daily activities, it's worth getting it assessed. Most back pain is mechanical and responds well to the right physiotherapy — waiting and hoping it resolves on its own is the slowest possible recovery strategy. Learn more about back pain treatment →

Can desk-related neck and shoulder pain actually be fixed?

Yes — and it's one of the most common things I treat. Years of forward-head posture, screen time and shoulder tension respond extremely well to a combination of manual therapy, deep neck flexor exercises and small ergonomic adjustments. Most desk workers see clear improvement within 3–4 sessions.

I had an MRI that showed a disc bulge — do I need surgery?

Almost certainly not. Research has shown that most asymptomatic adults over 30 have disc bulges and degenerative changes on MRI, which means these findings correlate poorly with pain. Surgery is genuinely indicated in only a small minority of cases. The DBC certified spinal rehabilitation programme is one of the most effective non-surgical alternatives available. Learn more about DBC →

How soon after a sports injury should I see a physiotherapist?

As soon as possible — ideally within the first week. The first 72 hours after an injury are when the most important early decisions get made, and getting them right is the difference between a quick return and a drawn-out recovery. Don't wait for the pain to "settle" — early assessment shortens recovery time significantly.
